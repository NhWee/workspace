Why this direction
------------------
On the current machine, the most realistic interactive fluid result is not a
full 3D Navier-Stokes air-water simulation. Full volumetric 3D CFD with foam,
spray, overturning waves, and high-resolution rendering is too expensive for
real-time work on an RTX 4060 Ti.

The practical high-realism direction is:

1. simulate the visible free surface
2. use a choppy multi-wave spectrum for ocean motion
3. add crest foam and spray
4. shade the surface with sun/sky lighting
5. keep the scene interactive in WebGL

This is the same basic strategy used by many real-time ocean renderers: spend
the GPU budget on the surface that the viewer sees.

Model
-----
The ocean surface is a 2.5D height/displacement field:

position(x, z, t) =
    base_position
    + horizontal_choppy_displacement
    + vertical_wave_height

The wave field is made from multiple Gerstner-like components:

height += amplitude * sin(k * dot(direction, position) - omega * t + phase)
horizontal displacement += steepness * amplitude * direction * cos(phase)

The components form a directional spectrum: a dominant swell is mixed with
weaker crossing waves and short wind ripples. Their wavelengths, directions,
and phases are intentionally varied so the surface does not repeat as evenly
spaced wave rows. Each component uses deep-water dispersion
(angular_frequency proportional to sqrt(gravity * wave_number)).

Foam is generated from:

1. sharp crests
2. steep slopes
3. moving vortex/wake centers

The foam mask is then broken into a drifting, multi-scale pattern. Dense
breaking crests stay coherent, while their lower-density edges fragment and
fade into moving patches. This avoids a uniform white layer and prevents
frame-to-frame random flicker.

Spray particles are emitted from high-foam breaking regions and then move
ballistically under gravity.

Controls
--------
Wind
Controls wave forcing and travel speed. Higher values make the ocean more
energetic.

Choppiness
Controls horizontal displacement. Higher values create sharper, more folded
wave crests.

Swell
Controls large-scale wave amplitude.

Foam
Controls crest/wake foam intensity.

Spray
Controls the amount of airborne spray particles.

Sun
Controls sunlight and highlight intensity.

Glint
Controls Fresnel-style edge reflection and sharp sun sparkle on steep wave
facets. Higher values make the surface look wetter and more reflective, but too
high can become glittery.

Material tuning
---------------
The water uses a moderately rough physical material and blue-tinted reflection.
Foam and high crests are cool blue-white rather than pure white, so the surface
keeps visible depth instead of reading as a uniformly bright solid.

Buttons
-------
Pause
Stops or resumes the viewer.

Storm preset
Sets wind, swell, foam, and spray higher while keeping Gerstner steepness below
the self-intersection range. It should look energetic without producing folded,
pillar-like wave geometry.

Calm preset
Sets lower-energy ocean parameters.

Spray Off / On
Toggles airborne spray.

Foam Off / On
Toggles foam coloring on the water surface.

Important note
--------------
The HTML uses Three.js from a CDN:

https://unpkg.com/three@0.160.0/build/three.min.js

So the first load needs internet access. After the browser caches it, repeated
loads are usually fast.

Limitations
-----------
This is not a full 3D fluid volume. It does not solve air-water interaction,
bubble entrainment, or true breaking-wave topology. It is designed to produce
the most realistic interactive water surface we can reasonably build in this
workspace and hardware setup.

Best next improvements
----------------------
